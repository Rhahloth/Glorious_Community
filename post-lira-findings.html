Published: April 2025 | Author: GCMHSC Research Unit

In a recent mental health data analysis conducted at secondary schools in Lira City, our team evaluated the emotional wellbeing of students, aiming to understand their mental health status, coping strategies, and access to professional support.

Key Demographics

The survey had a fairly balanced gender split with 47 females and 43 males. Ages ranged from 12 to 22, with the majority between 16 and 17 years old (mean: 16.61 years).

Emotional Health and Distress

Students frequently reported feeling stressed, overwhelmed, and anxious. About 34% had trouble concentrating, while 20% struggled with sleep. These issues are early warning signs of potential mental distress if unaddressed.

Self-Care Practices

A promising 87.6% of the students engage in self-care activities like meditation, physical exercise, and hobbies. Notably, most students who experienced recent life changes were more inclined to practice self-care.

Professional Help vs. Support Systems

Only 11.1% of the students sought professional help, and none reported it as effective ‚Äî signaling a significant gap in mental health service delivery. However, students who had support systems (family, friends, mentors) showed better mood outcomes and higher engagement in self-care.

Mood Outcomes

Among students with support systems, 63% reported elevated moods despite not accessing professional therapy. In contrast, those without support and who experienced major life events reported higher rates of irritability and depression.

Recommendations from Our Team

  • Improve Access to Mental Health Services: Schools should provide better counseling support with trained professionals.
  • Encourage Self-Care: Promote awareness and training on daily wellness routines that help manage emotional states.
  • Strengthen Peer and Family Support: Encourage strong student social circles and adult mentorship.
  • Conduct Regular Assessments: Periodic mental health screening can help identify and support at-risk students early.

This report highlights a critical need for schools, caregivers, and communities to collaborate in supporting the emotional and mental well-being of students in Uganda.
